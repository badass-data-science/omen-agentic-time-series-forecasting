# ts-deploy Ships It (or, How Our Heroine Taught Layer 3 to Stop Hedging and Commit to a Number)

*In which gradient-boosted trees gets an opinion about its own uncertainty for the first time, two mediocre guesses combined turn out to know more than either alone, and a two-row time series nearly takes the whole layer down over a frequency string.*

Two posts ago, `ts-analyst` learned to never say "significant" without saying "by how much." One post ago, `ts-forecaster` learned that "the error was lower" isn't an argument, and caught a genuinely broken hypothesis test on its way to proving it. Both of those layers spent their respective glow-ups being *graded* — every number they produce gets checked against a held-out window of real values, because that's the whole point of a backtest.

`ts-deploy` doesn't get that luxury. Layer 3's job is to retrain the chosen model on the *entire* series — no more held-out data, Layer 2 already spent that — and forecast into a future that, by definition, nobody can check yet. There is no ground truth to grade against. There is only a number, going out the door, that someone is going to use to decide how many mojitos to order for the Secret Lab™. For two whole rounds of this project's rigor pass, `ts-deploy` sat there mostly untouched, quietly forecasting away with none of the effect sizes, confidence intervals, or hypothesis tests its siblings had been collecting. Its turn has now come.

## The Problem With Shipping, Not Grading

Every other layer in this toolkit gets to check its work. `ts-analyst` runs a test and reports a p-value. `ts-forecaster` fits a model, forecasts a holdout window it already has the real answers for, and scores itself. `ts-deploy` fits a model and forecasts a window nobody has answers for *yet* — which means the usual trick of "here's how confident you should be, verified against reality" is off the table. All that's left is honesty about the forecast itself: how wide is the uncertainty, does the trajectory make sense given what the series has actually done before, and — if there's more than one reasonable candidate — how do you combine them without pretending the combination is more certain than it actually is?

That turned out to be three separate, real gaps, closed over three separate rounds this session, plus one bug that had been sitting in the code for months waiting for someone to hand it a short enough series to expose it.

## The Tools, One at a Time

**`forecast_naive`** used to be the one tool in this entire toolkit — across all five layers — with *zero* interval capability, ever, not even a fallback attempt. It now has an honest one: a textbook analytic interval (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos) built from the naive method's own in-sample residuals — one-step differences for flat naive, seasonal differences for seasonal naive — widening with the square root of elapsed steps (or completed seasonal cycles, for the seasonal variant). Run against the project's own synthetic data, the interval starts at a width of about 90 units on day one and balloons to over 330 units by day fourteen, which is exactly the point: a naive forecast that just repeats the last value has no actual model of the series behind it, so its honest uncertainty should look enormous next to a model that does. If there's fewer than two residuals to even estimate a standard deviation from, it falls back to point-only rather than reporting a fabricated interval — a real "can't compute this" state, not a zero pretending to be an answer.

**`forecast_ets`** and **`forecast_sarima`** kept their existing intervals (simulated and analytic, respectively) but picked up `aicc`, the same small-sample AIC correction `ts-forecaster` uses. Worth being precise about what it's *for* here: this refit happens on the full series, not the training split Layer 2 used, so the number is a diagnostic on the deployed model itself, not a rematch against Layer 2's candidate comparison. Reporting it without that caveat would have quietly implied a comparison that doesn't actually hold.

**`forecast_gradient_boosted_trees`** was the layer's most obvious remaining hole: a real prediction interval, for the first time in its life. Two extra `GradientBoostingRegressor` models, trained with `loss="quantile"` at the low and high ends, ride alongside the original point model — which triples this tool's compute cost, a tradeoff stated plainly rather than buried. The recursive forecasting loop still only ever follows the *point* model's own trajectory when building lag features for later steps; the two quantile models are evaluated against that same path rather than recursing on their own predictions, which would otherwise produce three diverging futures instead of one forecast with a band around it. Independently-fit quantile models can also just plain cross — the "low" one predicting higher than the "high" one, which is nonsense — so the two arrays get an elementwise min/max swap before anything is returned. And because the interval doesn't itself grow with GBT's signature compounding-error risk (each recursive step trusting the last one to have been roughly right), both `interval_note` and `caveat` say so explicitly. An interval that quietly overstated its own rigor here would have undercut the exact caveat this tool has been carrying since the day it shipped.

**`plausibility_check`**, now returned by every tool in this layer including the ensemble below, automates something `ts-deploy/SKILL.md` used to leave entirely to the agent's judgment: does this forecast's trajectory actually look plausible given what the series has done before? It compares the forecast's implied endpoint change against the empirical distribution of horizon-length changes the series has made historically, and separately flags whether the forecast's own min or max ever leaves the historical range entirely. Deliberately *not* a hypothesis test — there's no null distribution to reject, only "has this series done something like this move before" — because a genuine regime change can produce a forecast that's flagged as extreme and still turns out to be correct. The field is a prompt to look closer, not a verdict to hide behind.

**`forecast_ensemble`** is the newest tool outright: combine two or more of the above into one weighted forecast, for the situation where Layer 2 leaves more than one reasonable candidate and forcing a single winner would just be arbitrary. It fills a gap the `ts-forecaster` post named explicitly as unfinished business — combining candidates is this layer's job, not Layer 2's, since it's about producing a forecast to use rather than judging candidates in the abstract. Weights default to equal and needn't be pre-normalized, so raw inverse-error values straight from a Layer 2 comparison work without any manual arithmetic first.

## The Ensemble's Interval Got a Do-Over

The first version of `forecast_ensemble`'s combined interval did the obvious, lazy thing: average each component's lower bound, average each component's upper bound, done. It even said so honestly in its own `interval_note` — "not a statistically rigorous ensemble interval" — which is the kind of caveat that reads fine right up until someone asks "well, could it be better?" It could.

The current version converts each component's own interval width back into an implied standard deviation, combines those under an assumption that the components' errors are *independent* (`sqrt(sum(w_i² · σ_i²))`), and rebuilds the interval symmetrically around the weighted point forecast. This is a real, standard variance-combination technique — not a bound average wearing a nicer name. Run against two identical naive components at equal weight, the combined interval comes out to exactly `1/√2` (about 71%) the width of either component's own interval alone — a naive average of two *identical* intervals would obviously have just reproduced the same interval, so this is a visibly different, and mathematically correct, result: combining independent evidence genuinely does shrink uncertainty. The catch, stated as plainly in the code as everywhere else in this project's caveats: the independence assumption is optimistic, since every component was fit on the *same* series and shares real error structure that this formula can't see. The result is framed as a lower bound on the ensemble's true uncertainty, not a precise one — and it can legitimately come out narrower than any single component's own interval, which is the expected mathematical consequence of the independence assumption, not a bug to go clamp away.

| | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| `forecast_gradient_boosted_trees` interval | None, ever | Quantile-regression interval, ~3x compute cost |
| `forecast_naive` interval | None, ever | Analytic residual-based interval, widens with horizon |
| `forecast_ensemble` combined interval | Weighted average of bounds | Variance combination under an independence assumption |

While the ensemble's interval logic was being reworked, `forecast_gradient_boosted_trees` picked up one more thing: a bootstrap confidence interval on its own `feature_importances`, which used to be a bare point estimate sitting right next to a project's worth of fields that all got confidence intervals. Refitting on a hundred resamples of the training rows (not resampled errors — there's no "error" to resample for a feature-importance question, only what the model was actually fit on) turned out to be informative, not decorative: on the project's own synthetic weekly-seasonal data, `lag_7` came back with an importance of 0.622 but a confidence interval of `[0.44, 0.79]`, because `lag_7` and `lag_14` are effectively competing to explain the same weekly pattern, and which one "wins" a given resample isn't especially stable. A single point estimate would have quietly implied more certainty about *why* the model works than the model actually has.

## The Bug That Was Already There

Adding `forecast_naive`'s new interval meant writing a test for its point-only fallback — the case where there isn't enough history to estimate a residual standard deviation. The obvious way to trigger that: build a two-row series and check that the interval gracefully declines to exist.

It didn't gracefully decline. It crashed — not in the new interval code, but in `_future_dates`, a small shared helper that every forecast tool in this layer has been quietly calling since the day it shipped. Its job is simple: figure out the series' date frequency and build a date range continuing from the last observation, falling back to daily if the frequency can't be inferred. The fallback logic — `pd.infer_freq(df["date"]) or "D"` — looks airtight. It is airtight, for exactly one failure mode: an *ambiguous* frequency, where `infer_freq` politely returns `None` and the `or "D"` picks up the slack. It is not airtight for the failure mode of *too few dates to attempt inference at all*, where `infer_freq` doesn't return `None` — it raises a `ValueError` instead, sailing straight past the `or` entirely.

Nobody had ever called any `forecast_*` tool with a one- or two-row series before, so this had been sitting there, untested and unnoticed, since before this session started. It took a two-row edge case for a completely unrelated feature to finally ask the question nobody had asked. The fix is a three-line `try/except` around the inference call — but the honest lesson is smaller and less flattering than the fix: an `or` fallback only covers the failure mode you tested for, and this codebase had simply never tested for this one.

## Next Steps

- **The ensemble's independence assumption stays unaddressed, on purpose.** Estimating the actual correlation between two models' errors would require exactly the kind of backtest ground truth Layer 3 doesn't have — that's Layer 2's job, not this one's. A future version that accepts an optional empirical correlation estimate *from* a Layer 2 comparison, rather than assuming independence blindly, would be a real improvement, but it's a genuine design question about how far a "pure function of its own inputs" tool should reach into another layer's output, not a quick fix.
- **GBT's interval still doesn't know about its own compounding-error risk.** The quantile-regression interval described above is evaluated fresh at each recursive step against that step's own features — it doesn't widen the way a proper multi-step interval would to reflect the fact that step 20 is standing on the shoulders of nineteen previous guesses. A residual-bootstrap simulation of the whole recursive path, closer to how `forecast_ets` already derives its own interval, is the obvious next attempt, and a materially harder one to get right.

## Conclusion

`ts-deploy` spent this project's first two rounds of rigor sitting quietly on the sidelines, producing forecasts nobody was checking the confidence of. It's caught up now: every tool returns an interval or explains clearly why it can't, every forecast gets checked against the series' own history before anyone has to eyeball it, and combining two decent candidates is now a real, if honestly caveated, option instead of forcing a single winner by fiat. The most useful thing found along the way wasn't a new feature at all — it was a three-line date-inference bug that had been quietly waiting for someone to ask this layer to handle a series shorter than three rows.

Mojito inventory forecasting proceeds with slightly more honest error bars than before.
