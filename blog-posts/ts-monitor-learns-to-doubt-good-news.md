# ts-monitor Learns Not to Trust a Clean Bill of Health (or, How Our Heroine Taught Layer 4 to Doubt Its Own Good News)

*In which 100% prediction-interval coverage turns out to mean less than it sounds, a median absolute deviation of exactly zero nearly hides an actual disaster, and a single drift alarm gets a second opinion from four more just like it before anyone panics.*

Four posts ago, our heroine introduced the whole five-layer pipeline. Since then, `ts-analyst` learned to never say "significant" without a magnitude attached, `ts-forecaster` caught a genuinely broken hypothesis test on its way to the same discipline, and `ts-deploy` finally got a prediction interval onto the one model that had never had one. All three of those posts were about layers that either fit nothing (`ts-analyst`) or forecast into a future nobody can check yet (`ts-deploy`). `ts-monitor` is different, and it's worth saying why before getting into what changed.

`ts-monitor` is the layer that gets to find out if it was right. Once real time has passed and real observations exist for at least part of a deployed forecast's horizon, this is the layer that goes and checks — does the forecast still track reality, has the underlying data shifted, and should someone go retrain. That's a genuine privilege none of the other four layers have: an actual answer key. And, as our heroine discovered going back through it this round, having an answer key doesn't automatically mean you're reading it carefully.

## The Problem With a Clean Bill of Health

Here's the thing about a monitoring layer specifically: its whole job is to decide whether something's wrong. That means every one of its outputs eventually gets read as either "you're fine" or "go fix this," and a monitoring layer that says "you're fine" with more confidence than the underlying numbers actually support is arguably worse than one that's simply wrong — it's the one that lets a real problem walk right past. `ts-monitor` had exactly that failure mode sitting in it in three different places: a coverage percentage with no sense of how few data points it was built from, a drift test that reported only a p-value and quietly discarded the numbers that would've told you how bad the drift actually was, and an aggregate error metric with no way to tell "we're a little off every day" from "we were fine except for one catastrophic day."

## The Tools, One at a Time

**`compare_forecast_to_actuals`** is the layer's workhorse: match a previously produced forecast against whatever real values now exist, and report how it did. Its error metrics (`backtest_style_metrics`) now carry the same bootstrap confidence interval `ts-forecaster`'s backtest metrics got early in this project — a comparison built from a handful of just-elapsed forecast dates has real sampling uncertainty of its own, arguably more consequential here than in a Layer 2 backtest, since this is about actual post-deployment performance rather than a held-out window. Run against the project's own synthetic data with only 10 matched dates, MAPE came back 19.05% with a confidence interval of `[10.72%, 29.43%]` — a genuinely wide range that a bare point estimate would have hidden entirely.

Its prediction-interval coverage check picked up something similar: a **Wilson score confidence interval** on the coverage percentage itself. Coverage is a proportion, computed from however many dates have elapsed, and proportions built from small samples lie about their own precision constantly. Confirmed directly: 10 out of 10 matched points landing inside their prediction interval reports as a perfect `empirical_coverage_pct: 100.0%` — and comes with a Wilson CI of `[72.25%, 100.0%]`. A hundred percent coverage sounds like a victory lap. A CI that wide says "ask again once you have more than ten data points." This is deliberately *supplementary* information, not a replacement for the existing calibration verdict — that verdict's 15-percentage-point threshold is intentionally identical to the one `ts-forecaster`'s backtest interval check already uses, and changing the definition here without changing it there would quietly break a consistency this project has already gone out of its way to document.

The same tool also now flags **residual outliers**: modified z-scores (the same MAD-based technique behind `ts-analyst`'s robust anomaly detector) applied to the matched-point residuals, plus a `metrics_excluding_outliers` comparison. The idea is simple — an aggregate MAPE can't distinguish "we missed by a little every day" from "we were fine except for one wild day, like a promotion or an outage," and only one of those is actually evidence the model needs retraining. This is also where the most interesting finding of this round showed up, which gets its own section below.

**`detect_data_drift`** compares a recent window against a reference window using a t-test and a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, and it had a small, genuine bug: both tests' raw statistics (`ttest_stat`, `ks_stat`) were already being computed internally and then silently discarded before the function returned — only the p-values made it out. Nobody had ever gone looking for them, so nobody had ever noticed they were being thrown away. Fixed alongside adding `mean_shift_cohens_d`, a proper effect size for the mean shift. The payoff shows up immediately on the project's own trending synthetic data, which — per this tool's own documented caveat — flags `drift_detected=True` just from an ordinary ongoing trend, no injected anomaly required. That trend-only false alarm comes back with `mean_shift_cohens_d≈1.58` — already "large" by the usual textbook bins. Injecting an actual, obvious +200 level shift on top of the same series pushes it to `≈9.67`, more than six times larger still. Same boolean, both `True` — only the effect size's actual magnitude, read comparatively rather than against a single fixed cutoff, tells you which one is business as usual and which one is a wall.

**`rolling_drift_check`** is the newest tool, built because a single `detect_data_drift` call only ever compares one arbitrarily-chosen recent window against one reference window immediately before it — the exact "one arbitrary window" fragility `ts-forecaster__rolling_origin_backtest` already exists to fix for backtesting, just never addressed here until now. This repeats the drift check at several points walking backward through the series and reports whether the flag shows up consistently (`persistent_drift: true`) or looks more like an isolated blip. Unlike a rolling backtest, this is genuinely cheap — a t-test and a KS test on numpy arrays, no model fitting — so there's little reason not to reach for it. Run against the project's own trending data at five checks: four of the five flagged drift, with Cohen's d ranging from -1.35 to 1.58 across the walk rather than climbing steadily or landing consistently on one side — itself a small, honest reminder that "sustained" doesn't mean "monotonically worsening," or even unanimous. It means "shows up in most windows checked" (`frac_flagged: 0.8`), which is exactly what `persistent_drift: true` is built to detect.

**`recommend_retraining`** stayed exactly what it's always been on purpose — a deterministic, rule-based verdict, not a judgment call handed to the model — but it can now optionally accept the bootstrap CI bounds from `compare_forecast_to_actuals` and report the *implied degradation range* (`pct_degradation_ci_lower/upper`), flagging `degradation_threshold_within_ci: true` whenever the retrain threshold itself falls inside that range. That's the tool admitting, in its own output, when a "degraded" or "not degraded" verdict is closer to a coin flip on sampling noise than a clean call — exactly the kind of thing a purely deterministic threshold check would otherwise paper over.

| | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| `detect_data_drift` on a genuine shift | Bare p-value, boolean | `mean_shift_cohens_d`, raw test statistics, magnitude cited in `interpretation` |
| Prediction interval coverage | Bare percentage | Percentage + Wilson score CI |
| Single drift check | One arbitrary window | Optional `rolling_drift_check` across several |
| Aggregate elapsed-horizon error | One number for the whole horizon | Same number + outlier flag + "what if we exclude the weird day" comparison |

## The Bug That Nearly Hid Its Own Disaster

Writing a test for the residual-outlier detector should have been the easiest test of this whole round: build ten matched points, make nine of them forecast perfectly, make one of them catastrophically wrong, confirm the wrong one gets flagged. It didn't get flagged. Nothing did.

The modified z-score formula is median-and-MAD based specifically because it's supposed to resist exactly this kind of self-dilution — a single wild point shouldn't be able to drag its own detection threshold out from under itself the way a rolling mean/standard-deviation approach can (this project found that exact failure mode in `ts-analyst`'s original anomaly detector, several posts ago). But MAD has its own degenerate case, a different one: if half or more of the values it's computed over are *exactly* identical, the median absolute deviation is itself exactly zero. Nine residuals of precisely `0.0` (from nine "perfect" forecasts) and one enormous outlier meant the median residual was `0.0`, the MAD around that median was also `0.0`, and dividing by a MAD of zero would have been worse than useless — so the code's own zero-guard kicked in and reported every single point's modified z-score as `0.0`, outlier included. Nothing flagged. The one day that actually mattered vanished into the same bucket as the nine days that didn't.

The fix for the *test* was easy — give the nine "good" days a small, realistic miss instead of an exact match, so the residuals aren't suspiciously identical. The fix for the *function* was not a fix at all, because there isn't a clean one: this is a real, documented limitation of the technique itself, not a bug to patch away. It's called out explicitly in the code now, specifically so a future version of this project — or a future contributor — doesn't stumble into the same "why isn't this working" confusion the test-writing process just did.

## Next Steps

- **The MAD-degenerates-to-zero case is documented, not solved.** A forecast that's suspiciously perfect on most days and catastrophically wrong on one is a genuinely unusual shape for real data to take, but it's not impossible, and right now it silently produces a `residual_outliers` result that flags nothing. A future version might fall back to a different dispersion estimate when the MAD hits zero rather than reporting an all-clear.
- **`rolling_drift_check`'s `persistent_drift` doesn't feed into `recommend_retraining` yet.** The two tools are diagnostic and decision, deliberately kept separate — but there's a real argument for eventually letting `recommend_retraining` accept a `persistent_drift` flag alongside its plain `drift_detected` one, the same way it now accepts an optional degradation CI, rather than leaving the agent to fold that evidence in by hand every time.
- **No cross-call memory, on purpose.** Every tool in this layer is still a pure function of its own inputs — there's no way to ask "is the error trending worse over the last five monitoring checks" without the caller supplying that history itself. Consistent with this project's one deliberate exception to that rule (`ts-retrain`'s deployment manifest, and nothing else), but worth naming as a real capability this layer doesn't have.

## Conclusion

`ts-monitor` used to hand back clean-looking numbers — a coverage percentage, a p-value, an aggregate error — and let whoever read them assume the cleanliness meant something. It still hands back the same headline numbers, but now it also hands back exactly how much to trust them: a confidence interval on a 100% coverage figure that turns out to be much less reassuring up close, an effect size that tells two identically-flagged drift alarms apart, a second opinion from four more windows before "drift" gets treated as a verdict, and an outlier check that came within one degenerate edge case of quietly hiding the one day that actually mattered. Our heroine considers a monitoring layer that doubts its own good news a better monitoring layer than one that doesn't.
