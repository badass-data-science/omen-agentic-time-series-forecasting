# ts-analyst Gets a Statistics Degree (or, How Our Heroine Taught Layer 1 to Stop Guessing and Start Citing Its Sources)

*In which a rolling z-score gets caught red-handed diluting its own evidence, a uniform threshold turns out to have been wrong the whole time, and nobody is allowed to say "significant" without also saying "by how much."*

Last time, our heroine introduced the full five-layer assembly line: explore, backtest, deploy, monitor, retrain, with one very suspicious foreman at the end who refuses to redeploy anything without being asked twice. That post covered the whole pipeline at cruising altitude. This one lands the plane specifically on **Layer 1, `ts-analyst`** — the tools that look at a series before anyone's allowed to fit a model to it — because Layer 1 quietly turned into the part of the toolkit our heroine is proudest of, and also the part where she found an actual bug in her own reasoning, which is the kind of thing you either bury or write a blog post about.

She wrote the blog post.

## The Problem With "Yes" and "No"

Here's the thing about classical statistical tests: they're built to answer yes/no questions — is this stationary, is this lag significant, is this point an outlier — and yes/no questions are exactly the kind of question that quietly discards the most useful part of the answer. A series can clear `p < 0.05` on a stationarity test while reverting back to its mean so slowly that the "stationarity" is a technicality nobody building a 30-day forecast should care about. An anomaly detector can flag a point as "over threshold" without telling you whether it barely crossed the line or blew through it by an order of magnitude. Two findings that both say "significant" can be wildly different findings wearing the same label.

Our heroine's fix, applied uniformly across every diagnostic tool in `ts-analyst`: never report a verdict without also reporting a magnitude, and never report a magnitude without also reporting how sure you are about it. Effect sizes and confidence intervals, everywhere, whether or not anyone asked for them.

## The Tools, One at a Time

**`basic_stats`** got the gentlest treatment: length, date range, missing values, mean/std/min/max, same as always, plus a confidence interval on the mean via Student's t. Nothing dramatic here except a small correctness detail our heroine insisted on: the standard error uses the count of *non-missing* values, not the raw row count. Get that wrong and a series with gaps in it quietly reports a narrower interval than the data actually supports.

**`check_stationarity`** got the most thorough overhaul. It used to run just the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test — null hypothesis: unit root, i.e. non-stationary. Now it also runs the KPSS test, whose null hypothesis is the *opposite*: stationary. Running both together and reading them jointly is standard practice in real econometrics, not redundancy, because each test's blind spot is roughly the other's strength; the tool now returns a genuine four-way readout (both agree stationary, both agree non-stationary, or either flavor of disagreement, each with its own specific guidance). On top of that, both tests get an effect size: ADF's comes from fitting the classic mean-reversion regression and reporting the estimated speed of reversion as a half-life, in periods, with a confidence interval; KPSS's comes from expressing its own test statistic as a multiple of its 5% critical value, which matters because KPSS's p-value is coarse — `statsmodels` interpolates it from four lookup-table points and clips it at the edges, so two very different series can both report `p=0.01` with the p-value giving you no way to tell them apart. The effect size doesn't have that problem.

**`seasonal_decomposition_summary`** is unchanged in spirit but got a new sibling: **`detect_seasonality_period`**, because the old tool had an honest design flaw — you had to already know the seasonal period before you could ask it anything. The new tool finds a candidate period for you, via a periodogram and a formal significance test for hidden periodicity (Fisher's g-test, 1929, for anyone counting how far back this toolkit's statistical ancestry goes), ranked by the share of total spectral power each candidate accounts for. Run against the project's own synthetic demand data, it correctly surfaces a period of ~7 (accounting for 36% of spectral power) as the top *sensible* candidate — while separately, correctly, flagging that the single globally strongest frequency in the raw periodogram actually corresponds to a period near the full length of the series, which is just the trend showing up where a naive tool would have called it "seasonality."

**`acf_pacf_summary`** is where our heroine found the bug. The original significance threshold for flagging an autocorrelation lag as notable was a single number, `1.96/sqrt(n)`, applied uniformly to every lag. This number is only actually correct for lag 1. Past that, the proper standard error (Bartlett's formula) grows with the cumulative autocorrelation of every earlier lag — because statistically, testing lag *k* is like asking "does this look like more structure than an MA(k-1) model would already predict," and a series with strong short-lag autocorrelation needs a *wider* bar for anything past it to count as new information. `statsmodels` computes this correctly and had been sitting right there the whole time. Once switched over, the ranking of "most significant lag" on the project's own test data flipped: the old uniform threshold ranked lag 7 (the real weekly seasonality) as strongest; the corrected per-lag threshold ranks lag 1 as strongest instead, because lag 1's Bartlett standard error is the tightest of any lag — nothing earlier is inflating it. Neither answer is wrong, exactly, but only one of them is the statistically correct one, and it wasn't the one the tool had been giving out.

**`detect_anomalies_zscore`** is where our heroine found a second, more embarrassing bug, this time by accident, while writing a test. The rolling z-score works by comparing each point to a local rolling mean and standard deviation — except the rolling window used to compute "local standard deviation" includes the anomalous point itself. Inject a spike five standard deviations tall into a 14-point window, and that window's own std balloons to accommodate it, which quietly shrinks the z-score of the very point that caused the inflation. Confirmed directly: a synthetic spike of +500 on a series that normally hovers around 200 scored a z-score of 3.44 — barely over the default threshold of 3.0, for a point that any human glancing at the chart would call unmissable.

**`detect_anomalies_robust_zscore`** exists because of that finding specifically. Same job, different arithmetic: rolling median and median absolute deviation (MAD) instead of rolling mean and standard deviation, rescaled into a "modified z-score" (Iglewicz & Hoya, 1993). Medians and MADs are far less sensitive to a single outlier sitting inside their own window — the median of fourteen mostly-normal points barely moves if only one of them is wild. Run against the exact same injected spike: modified z-score of 17.26. Same data, same "how anomalous is this point" question, a five-times-more-honest answer.

**`detect_changepoints`** is the one genuinely new capability, answering a question none of the other tools were built to ask: not "is this one point weird," but "did the series' actual *level* permanently shift at some point." A single huge spike and a lasting, modest step up in the baseline are both interesting, but they're different problems requiring different responses, and conflating them is how you end up either ignoring a real regime change or panicking over a one-off. Under the hood: binary segmentation with a standardized CUSUM statistic, and — because working out the exact closed-form critical values for that statistic correctly is the kind of thing our heroine did not trust herself to get right from memory — a seeded permutation test for significance instead, same philosophy the deployment layer already uses for its own simulated forecast intervals. Each detected break comes with Cohen's d as an effect size and, importantly, an explicit disclaimer: this class of algorithm doesn't give an exact global significance guarantee across the whole set of breaks it finds, only a local test at each individual split. Our heroine would rather ship an honest asterisk than a false sense of precision.

## A Before/After, Because Showing Beats Telling

| Tool | Old behavior | New behavior |
|---|---|---|
| `acf_pacf_summary` | Uniform threshold ranks lag 7 as strongest | Bartlett CI correctly ranks lag 1 as strongest |
| `detect_anomalies_zscore` | +500 spike scores z=3.44 (self-diluted) | Robust version scores modified-z=17.26 on the same spike |
| `check_stationarity` | ADF only, bare p-value | ADF + KPSS, joint verdict, effect size, confidence interval on both |

## Next Steps

- **An exact Fisher's g-test p-value, not the conservative upper-bound approximation.** The current implementation uses the standard first-term approximation of the full alternating-series formula (the same one the GeneCycle R package uses), which is safe but not maximally precise for borderline cases.
- **A false-discovery correction across the full set of detected changepoints.** Binary segmentation's local per-split p-values are honest about what they are, but a proper family-wise or FDR correction across the whole reported set would be a genuine improvement over "here's a disclaimer, trust the individual numbers."
- **Bootstrapped confidence intervals for the seasonal-strength metrics**, which currently have no CI at all — unlike everything else in Layer 1 at this point, which is starting to feel like an oversight specifically because everything else got one.

## Conclusion

`ts-analyst` no longer just says yes or no. It says yes or no, by how much, and how sure it is — and in at least two documented cases, saying "by how much" is what caught the tool quietly lying to itself. Our heroine considers this a good trade for the extra JSON fields.
